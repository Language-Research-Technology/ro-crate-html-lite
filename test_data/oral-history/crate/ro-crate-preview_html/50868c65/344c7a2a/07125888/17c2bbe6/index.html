Description This project records the stories of power industry workers and their families relating to Victoria’s first brown coal generated power station at Yallourn that was established in 1921 and the State Electricity Commission of Victoria's model company town of Yallourn which was completely razed to the ground by the 1980s to allow forfurther mining. The project also records the memories of the Yallourn Medical and Hospital Society, one of the first contributory health schemes to operate in Australia. In 1926 Dr James Moore Andrew took the position of the Society’s first resident medical officer and with his wife Catherine Dorothy Andrew established a family at Yallourn, practising at Yallourn Hospital and lived in Yallourn until his death in 1972. Twenty individual audio interviews were carried out with Yallourn workers and recorded in their homes along with photographs. These are now stored in mp3 audio files on Old Gippstown computers.

Collection characteristics

Purpose 1. Preservation of the history of the region and especially of the township of Yallourn which was removed in the 1980s to make way for additional coal mining 2. Reinvigoration of displays will lead to further visits to Old Gippsland and to increased tourist spending in the region 3. Increased knowledge of the power industry, the township of Yallourn and surrounding towns, the provision of health care for SECV workers and their families, and of labour and migrant history 4. Increased sense of pride in heritage and identification with that past, as well as increased sense of unity through knowledge of shared background 5. Retention of resources within the community, with recordings being lodged in the Yallourn house at Old Gippstown and on the Old Gippstown buildings website, as well as at Moe and District Historical Society. This will strengthen the sense of ownership of the past and ensure ease of access by local residents and researchers.
